OVERVIEW

RESPONSIBILITIES

As an aerospace engineer, you will have to know how to build and test prototypes using computer aided design (CAD) software. They will also have to be able to analyze the results to improve it in some way. This could include either the physical structure, the aerodynamics, or both. These engineers might have to research types of fuels and ways to reduce engine emissions. They will have to manage projects along with managing and supervising a team of other engineers and technicians. They will be able to develop new tools and softwares for the crafts to use.

WORK ENVIRONMENT

Aerospace engineers operate within a varied work landscape, dictated by their specialization. They might be stationed in factories overseeing the manufacturing process, in offices conducting design and analysis tasks, or even on aircraft or in hangars for maintenance and testing. In roles requiring hands-on work, engineers often wear protective clothing to ensure safety. These diverse environments underscore the multifaceted nature of their profession, demanding adaptability and expertise across a spectrum of settings to drive innovation and excellence in aerospace engineering.

IMPACT

Aerospace engineers are able to develop satellites which provide information for military communications, positioning and navigations, weather forecasts, television broadcasts, and internet delivery. They are also able to create new ways to improve the engineering workspace by developing new tools and machinery. Aerospace engineers are one of the main reasons we are able to have man set foot on the Moon and land rovers on far away planets like Mars. They also have an impact on our national security by using surveillance, reconnaissance, and military operations to defend against potential threats.
